Bio

An ethnomusicologist by training, I teach and conduct research at UvA’s Department of Media Studies and Amsterdam School for Heritage, Memory and Material Culture (AHM). My research focuses on music, migration and Dutch colonial history. Broadly, I am interested in how music and sound shape experiences of self and other (in other words: who am I, what makes me human and connects me to other humans, and how do I relate to the human and nonhuman others with whom I share the world). My PhD research traced musical and sonic practices from Indonesia to Suriname and the Netherlands. My current research project, Audibilities of the Colonial Past, investigates ethnographic sound archives in the Netherlands within the framework of modernity/coloniality (referencing the work of Aníbal Quijano and Walter Mignolo) and traces ways of thinking about music, sound, data and human difference from the colonial past to the present.


My teaching has covered a wide variety of topics, from music to archives to ethnographic film to AI and creativity; and I supervise theses in UvA’s Masters programs in Archival and Information Studies, Film Studies, and Preservation and Presentation of the Moving Image (all within the Department of Media Studies). Across my teaching activities, I aim to foster critical thinking about how the perceptions and ideas that shape our meaningful experiences—such as practices of listening and notions of difference, culture, creativity, identity, technology, and the past—are subjective, contingent, and historically formed.


I studied ethnomusicology at Columbia University (PhD, 2020) and information studies at the University of Texas at Austin’s School of Information (MSIS, 2011). Before that, I studied anthropology and music composition at Oberlin College and Conservatory (BA, 2007).

Research

Since the invention of portable sound recording technologies around the turn of the 20th century, sonic specimens have been collected from “the field” and used as a source of knowledge to attempt to answer big questions about human difference, the origins of creativity, and the nature of humanity. Early 20th-century musicologists traversed colonial empires and the European countryside, collecting sonic data and imagining a vast archive of sound recordings big enough to provide the empirical evidence to answer such profound questions. In the present age of digitization and datafication, the imaginary of a very big archive of sonic data can potentially be realized; but, given more recent critical reflections on colonial archives, data collection, and knowledge production, have modes of understanding the relationship between sound, evidence, and human difference changed as well? What kind of evidence can historical sound recording collections provide for different kinds of users and purposes today—including critical scholarship, restitution and reparation, and artistic reappropriation?


Audibilities of the Colonial Past: Dutch Sound Archives as History, Heritage and Data is a four-year research project (2023-28) funded by the Dutch Research Council (NWO). In this project, I conduct historical research into three case studies of ethnographic sound recording collections from the context of Dutch colonial science and connect this history to a variety of contemporary practices of producing knowledge from recorded sound.

About this Website

The images are of 17th-century Dutch paintings in the genre known as the “bird concert” (vogelconcert). Depicting groups of birds from different climates gathered together to sing Western harmonies from a notated score, this painting tradition touches on themes of culture and nature, sound and listening, difference and coloniality, and representation.
